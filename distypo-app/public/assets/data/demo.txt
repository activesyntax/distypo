The morning fog clung to the harbor long after sunrise. the old lighthouse keeper had been watching the horizon since before dawn, his weathered coat pulled tight against the chill. Clouds gathered slowly in the west, heavy and low. fishermen worked quietly at the docks, mending their nets with the patience of men who had done it a thousand times before.

The smell of salt and diesel drifted across the pier. A young boy sat on the edge of the quay, dangling his feet above the dark water , watching a cormorant dry its wings on a buoy nearby. Seagulls circled overhead, calling to each other in short, urgent bursts. A sharp wind picked up around ten, scattering the gulls and sending loose ropes snapping against the masts. Locals said the storm would reach the coast by evening.

The  sky had turned the color of old pewter by early afternoon. Men moved with quiet urgency between the boats and the storage sheds, hauling crates and coiling lines. Most of them had already pulled their boats ashore, and the harbor felt emptier than usual. Only the lighthouse keeper remained at his post, unmoved, still watching the west.
